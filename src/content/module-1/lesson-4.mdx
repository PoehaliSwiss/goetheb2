## 1. üìò Deep Theory: The "People vs. Things" Split

In German, many verbs are married to a specific preposition (*warten auf*, *tr√§umen von*).
How you refer to the object depends strictly on whether it is a **Living Being (Person/Pet)** or a **Thing/Concept**.

### Case A: Living Beings (Person)

You must use **Preposition + Pronoun**.

* *Question:* **Auf wen** wartest du? (Who are you waiting for?)
* *Answer:* Ich warte **auf ihn** / **auf Maria**.

### Case B: Things/Concepts (Sache)

You must use the **Wo-** (Question) and **Da-** (Answer) compounds.

* *Question:* **Worauf** wartest du? (What are you waiting for?)
* *Answer:* Ich warte **darauf** / **auf den Bus**.

**The Vowel Rule:**
If the preposition starts with a vowel (an, auf, √ºber, in, aus), you insert an extra **-r-**.

* *da* + *auf* = **darauf**.
* *wo* + *an* = **woran**.
* *da* + *mit* (consonant) = **damit** (no r).

## 4. üíé Professional Grammar: The "Placeholder" Function

This is crucial for **Schreiben Teil 1**.
Often, the "Thing" you are talking about is **a whole sentence** (Nebensatz).
You put the *Da-word* in the main clause as an anchor/placeholder.

* *Structure:* **Main Clause (with Da-word)**, **Subordinate Clause (dass/zu...)**.

**Example:**

* *Bad B1:* Ich freue mich, dass du kommst.
